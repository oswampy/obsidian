This image illustrates a **Dehydrohalogenation reaction** (specifically an $E2$ elimination) occurring on a substituted cyclohexane ring.


The reaction shows a substituted cyclohexane being treated with **alcoholic KOH (Alc. KOH)**, which acts as a strong base.

- **Reactant:** A cyclohexane ring with three substituents:
    
    - An **Ethyl (Et)** group and a **Hydrogen (H)** atom at one $\beta$ position.
        
    - A **Chlorine (Cl)** atom on a wedge (solid bond) at the $\alpha$ position.
        
    - A **Methyl (Me)** group on a wedge and a **Hydrogen (H)** atom on a dash at the other $\beta$ position.
        
- **Reagent:** Alcoholic KOH (Alc. KOH).
    
- **Mechanism:** This is an $E2$ elimination. For $E2$ to occur in cyclic systems, the leaving group (Cl) and the $\beta$-hydrogen must be **anti-periplanar** (180° apart).
    
    - Since the Cl is on a **wedge** (solid), it can only eliminate with a hydrogen that is on a **dash**.
        
    - The hydrogen at the bottom $\beta$ position is on a dash, making it the preferred site for elimination.
        
- **Product:** The resulting molecule is a cyclohexene derivative where the double bond has formed between the $\alpha$ carbon and the bottom $\beta$ carbon, following Zaitsev's rule and stereochemical requirements.

### Mechanism: Rearrangement in carbocations

### Reaction Mechanism

The process shows the transformation of a **primary carbocation** into a **secondary carbocation**:

$$CH_3 - \underset{\text{H}}{\overset{\text{H}}{\overset{|}{\underset{|}{\text{C}_{\alpha}}}}} - \overset{\oplus}{\text{C}}\text{H}_2 \xrightarrow{1,2\text{-}H^\ominus \text{ shift}} CH_3 - \overset{\oplus}{\underset{\text{H}}{\underset{|}{\text{C}}}} - \text{CH}_3$$

 Stability Comparison

The driving force for this rearrangement is the increase in stability provided by **hyperconjugation**:

| **Feature**          | **Initial State**   | **Final State**       |
| -------------------- | ------------------- | --------------------- |
| **Carbocation Type** | Primary ($1^\circ$) | Secondary ($2^\circ$) |
| **Alpha Hydrogens**  | $2\alpha\text{-H}$  | $6\alpha\text{-H}$    |
| **Stability**        | **Less stable**     | **More stable**       |

### Important Points Related to Dehydration of Alcohols

The dehydration of alcohols typically follows an $E1$ mechanism where the following rules apply:

1. **Carbocation Formation:** A carbocation intermediate is formed during the process.
    
2. **Rate Determining Step (R.D.S):** The formation of the carbocation is the slowest step, and thus the R.D.S..
    
3. **Reactivity (R.O.R):** The Rate of Reaction (R.O.R) or reactivity is directly proportional to the **stability of the carbocation** formed.
    

**Comparing Reactivity Towards Dehydration:**

The more stable the resulting carbocation(after dehydration/shifting) (based on $\alpha$-hydrogen count), the faster the alcohol will dehydrate:

$$\text{Tertiary alcohol } (9\alpha\text{-H}) > \text{ Secondary alcohol } (6\alpha\text{-H}) > \text{ Primary alcohol } (3\alpha\text{-H})$$

### 1. Formation of the Primary Carbocation

The reaction begins with the protonated alcohol losing a water molecule under acidic conditions and heat ($H^+ / \Delta$).

- **Intermediate:** A primary carbocation ($1^\circ$) is initially formed: $CH_3-CH_2-CH_2-CH_2^+$.
    

### 2. Hydride Shift (Rearrangement)

Primary carbocations are highly unstable, so a **1,2-hydride shift** occurs to create a more stable secondary carbocation ($2^\circ$).

- A hydrogen atom (with its electrons) moves from the adjacent carbon to the positive center.
    
- **New Intermediate:** Secondary carbocation: $CH_3-CH_2-CH^+-CH_3$.

### 3. Elimination and Product Formation

From the secondary carbocation, protons can be removed from two different $\alpha$-carbons (labeled in red in the image) to form alkenes.

#### **Major Product: But-2-ene**

- **Mechanism:** Removal of a proton from the internal $CH_2$ group.
    
- **Stability:** This product has **$6 \alpha\text{-H}$** atoms (three from each terminal methyl group).
    
- **Rule:** Per **Zaitsev's Rule**, the more substituted alkene is the **Major** product due to increased hyperconjugation.
#### **Minor Product: But-1-ene**

- **Mechanism:** Removal of a proton from the terminal methyl group.
    
- **Stability:** This product has only **$2 \alpha\text{-H}$** atoms (from the adjacent $CH_2$ group).
    
- **Result:** It is the less substituted and therefore the **Minor** product.
    

### Summary of Products

|**Product Name**|**Structure**|**α-Hydrogens**|**Yield Status**|
|---|---|---|---|
|**But-2-ene**|$CH_3-CH=CH-CH_3$|6|**Major**|
|**But-1-ene**|$CH_2=CH-CH_2-CH_3$|2|**Minor**|

### Mechanism: Addition of Hydrogen Halide ($HX$)

 **Step 1: Protonation of the Alkene**

The alkene acts as a nucleophile ($NU$) and attacks the electrophilic proton ($H^{+}$) of the hydrogen halide ($HX$). The $H^{+}$ adds to the terminal carbon to form the most stable carbocation possible (Markovnikov's rule).

**Chemical Equation:**

$$R-CH=CH_{2} + H-X \longrightarrow R-\overset{+}{C}H-CH_{3} + X^{-}$$

 **Step 2: Carbocation Rearrangement (Optional)**

 because a **carbocation intermediate is formed**, the molecule may undergo a hydride or methyl shift to form a more stable (e.g., secondary to tertiary) carbocation before the next step.

**Step 3: Nucleophilic Attack**

The halide ion ($X^{-}$) attacks the positively charged carbon atom to form the final alkyl halide product.

**Final Product:**

$$R-CH(X)-CH_{3}$$



 **Key Differences from Halogen Addition ($X_{2}$)**

|**Feature**|**Halogen Addition (X2​)**|**Hydrohalogenation (HX)**|
|---|---|---|
|**Intermediate**|Cyclic Halonium Ion|**Carbocation**|
|**Rearrangement**|No (ring is too rigid)|**Yes** (possible if it increases stability)|
|**Stereochemistry**|Always **Anti**|Mixed (Syn and Anti possible)|

## **3) Addition of Halogens**

Alkenes react with halogens upon mixing in an inert solvent like $CCl_4$.

$$CH_3 - CH = CH_2 + Br_2 \xrightarrow{CCl_4} CH_3 - \underset{\text{Br}}{\text{CH}} - \underset{\text{Br}}{\text{CH}_2}$$

- **Observation:** The **Reddish Brown** color of Bromine turns **colorless** (Test for unsaturation).

#Q ![[Pasted image 20260206202502.png]]
### Mechanism:
![[Pasted image 20260206202035.png]]


 **Step 1: Formation of the Cyclic Halonium Ion**

The electron-rich pi ($\pi$) bond of the alkene attacks one of the halogen atoms ($X-X$). This induces a dipole in the halogen molecule, causing the leaving halogen to depart as a halide ion ($X^{-}$). Simultaneously, the halogen atom being attacked shares its lone pair back with the other carbon atom to form a stable, three-membered ring.

**Chemical Equation:**

$$R-CH=CH_{2} + X-X \longrightarrow \text{[Cyclic Halonium Ion]} + X^{-}$$

 **Step 2: Nucleophilic Attack (Anti-Addition)**

The remaining halide ion ($X^{-}$) acts as a nucleophile. It attacks one of the carbons of the cyclic halonium ion from the **opposite side** (backside attack) of the halogen bridge. This causes the ring to open.

- **Stereochemistry:** Because the nucleophile must attack from the side opposite the bridge(lone pair repulsion otherwise), the two halogen atoms end up on opposite faces of the molecule. This is known as **anti-addition**.

 **Key Features**

- **Intermediate:** A cyclic halonium ion (e.g., bromonium or chloronium ion).
    
- **Regioselectivity:** The nucleophile typically attacks the more substituted carbon because it carries more partial positive charge.
    
- **Stereospecificity:** Exclusively **anti-addition**.

## 8) Addition of Sulphuric Acid

This reaction uses **cold concentrated $H_{2}SO_{4}$** to produce alky\ hydrogen sulfates.
![[Pasted image 20260206210636.png]]
### **Mechanism Steps**

1. **Reagent Dissociation:** Sulfuric acid acts as the source of the electrophile: $H_{2}SO_{4} \longrightarrow H^+ + HSO_{4}^-$ (often written as $^-OSO_{3}H$).
    
2. **Formation of Carbocation:** Similar to the $HX$ reaction, the $H^+$ adds to the terminal carbon of the propene, forming an isopropyl carbocation.
    
3. **Nucleophilic Attack:** The bisulfate ion ($^-OSO_{3}H$) attacks the carbocation.
    

**Final Product:**

The product is an **alkyl hydrogen sulfate**. In the case of propene (shown in the image), it forms isopropyl hydrogen sulfate.

**Chemical Equation:**

$$CH_{3}-CH=CH_{2} \xrightarrow{H_{2}SO_{4}} CH_{3}-\overset{+}{C}H-CH_{3} \xrightarrow{^-OSO_{3}H} CH_{3}-CH(OSO_{3}H)-CH_{3}$$
