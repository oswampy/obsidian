###  Preparation of Alkynes

## 1. From Carbides 
### a)Calcium Carbide ($CaC_{2}$)

This is an industrial-scale preparation method.

1. **Quicklime** ($CaO$) is obtained by heating limestone ($CaCO_{3}$):
    
    $$CaCO_{3} \xrightarrow{\Delta} CaO + CO_{2}$$
    
2. **Calcium Carbide** is prepared by heating quicklime with coke ($C$):
    
    $$CaO + 3C \longrightarrow CaC_{2} + CO$$
    
3. **Ethyne** is produced by the reaction of calcium carbide with water:
    
    $$CaC_{2} + 2H_{2}O \longrightarrow Ca(OH)_{2} + HC \equiv CH$$


### **b) From Magnesium Carbide**

Magnesium carbide (**Mg2C3**) dissociates into magnesium ions and a specific carbide ion ($C_3^{4-}$), which then reacts to form Propyne.

**Step 1: Dissociation**

$$Mg_2C_3 \longrightarrow 2Mg^{2+} + C_3^{4-} \text{ (as } C^{3-}—C \equiv C^- \text{)}$$

**Step 2: Reaction with Hydroxyl and Hydrogen Ions**

- **Magnesium side:**
    $$2Mg^{2+} \xrightarrow{4OH^-} 2Mg(OH)_2$$
- **Carbide side:**
    $$C^{3-}—C \equiv C^- \xrightarrow{4H^+} CH_3—C \equiv CH$$
## 2. From Vicinal/Geminal Dihalides

Vicinal/geminal dihalides react with alcoholic $KOH$ and then sodamide ($NaNH_{2}$) to form alkynes via dehydrohalogenation.

1. **First Step:** Treatment with alcoholic $KOH$ removes one molecule of $HX$ to form a vinylic halide.
    
    $$CH_{2}Br-CH_{2}Br + \text{alc. } KOH \longrightarrow CH_{2}=CHBr$$
    
2. **Second Step:** Treatment with **Sodamide** ($NaNH_{2}$). Since alcoholic $KOH$ is not strong enough to carry out the second elimination, the more powerful base sodamide is used.
    
    $$CH_{2}=CHBr \xrightarrow{NaNH_{2}} HC \equiv CH$$

## Aromatic Hydrocarbons

AKA **Arenes** (known for their pleasant smell).

- **Benzenoids:** Compounds having a benzene ring.
    
- **Non-Benzenoids:** Aromatic compounds without a benzene ring.
    

### Aromaticity (Hückel's Rule):

1. Planar
    
2. Cyclic
    
3. Conjugation (delocalized $\pi$ electrons)
    
4. $(4n + 2)\pi$ electrons, where $n \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, \dots\}$

## Chemical Properties

### 1) Electrophilic Substitution ($S_{E}$)

$E^{+}$ (Electrophiles) like nucleophilic sites (electron-rich areas).
### i) Nitration
Reagent:
**Reagent:** $\text{conc. } HNO_3 + \text{conc. } H_2SO_4$
**Electrophile formed:** $NO_2^+$ (Nitronium ion)
#### **Mechanism: Formation of the Electrophile**

1. **Nitric Acid acting as a Lewis Base:**
    
    The $OH$ group in nitric acid ($HO-NO_2$) uses its lone pair of electrons to attack a proton ($H^+$) provided by the sulfuric acid.
    
    - **Equation:** $HO-NO_2 \xrightarrow{H^+} H-\overset{+}{O}(H)-NO_2$
        
    - **Note:** In this step, $HNO_3$ acts as a **Lewis Base**.
        
2. **Dehydration/Cleavage:**
    
    The protonated nitric acid intermediate is unstable. The bond between the oxygen and nitrogen breaks , releasing a water molecule ($H_2O$).
    
3. **Result:**
    
    This process generates the **Nitronium ion** ($NO_2^+$), which serves as the active electrophile for the nitration process. Which attacks the Benzene Ring

> **Key takeaway:** In the presence of concentrated sulfuric acid, nitric acid surprisingly acts as a base to generate the nitronium ion needed for electrophilic aromatic substitution.
### ii) Halogenation
 **The Reagents:**
$Halogen + anhy FeX_{3}$
#### **Mechanism:Generation of the Electrophile**

- **Formation of the Complex:** The halogen molecule ($X-X$) interacts with the iron atom in the Lewis acid ($FeX_3$). The iron is electron-deficient and accepts a lone pair from one of the halogen atoms.
    
- **Polarization and Cleavage:** As indicated by the curved green arrow in the image, the electron density shifts toward the iron. This weakens the $X-X$ bond, eventually leading to heterolytic cleavage.
    
- **The Reactive Species:** This results in the formation of:
    
    - **$X^+$ (Halonium Ion):** A highly reactive electrophile that can now attack the electron-rich benzene ring.
        
    - **$[FeX_4]^-$:** A temporary complex that later helps restore aromaticity by removing a proton from the ring.
        



### **iii)Sulphonation**

Sulphonation is the process of attaching a sulfonic acid functional group ($-SO_3H$) to a molecule.
 **Reagents:**
1. **Conc. $H_2SO_4$:** Concentrated sulfuric acid.
    
2. **Fuming $H_2SO_4$:** Sulfuric acid containing extra dissolved $SO_3$.
    
3. **Oleum ($H_2S_2O_7$):** Specifically, a mixture of $SO_3 + H_2SO_4$.
    

#### **Mechanism: Generating The Electrophile**

- **Active Electrophile:** **$SO_3$** (Sulfur trioxide).
    
- Even though $SO_3$ is a neutral molecule, it acts as a powerful electrophile because the sulfur atom is highly electron-deficient due to the three highly electronegative oxygen atoms surrounding it.

## Special Cases and Effects

### Excess Reagent

If an **excess of $E^{+}$ reagent** is used, further substitution takes place and other H-atoms of benzene also get replaced.

- **Example:** Benzene with excess $Cl_{2}$ in the presence of Anhydrous $AlCl_{3}$.
        $$C_{6}H_{6} + 6Cl_{2} \xrightarrow{\text{Anhyd. } AlCl_{3}} C_{6}Cl_{6} + 6HCl$$
        

### Ring Activators

**$+M / +R$ effect:** The electron pair goes inside the ring, increasing electron density at Ortho and Para positions.(creates negative charges at ortho and para)

**Activating: OH,NH2,COOR,NHCOR,etc
Deactivating: Halogen(X) (-I >+M)**
	it also allows electrons to go inside the ring but it pull more electron density than it pushes hence it is deactivating
- **Example:** Phenol reacting with $CH_{3}Cl$ (Alkylation).
    
    - **Major product:** Para-cresol (due to less steric hindrance).
    - **Minor product:** Ortho-cresol.
        
-M/-R effect: The electron pair goes outside the ring(create positive charges at ortho and para as electron density leaves)
- strong deactivating 
- ex: NO2

 **Step 1: Friedel-Crafts Alkylation**

The first step is the reaction of benzene with $CH_3-Cl$ and anhydrous $AlCl_3$ to form **toluene**.
- **Purpose:** This introduces the methyl group ($-CH_3$) onto the benzene ring.
- **Electrophile:** The reactive species is the methyl carbocation, $\overset{\oplus}{C}H_3$.


 **Step 2: Nitration**

Once toluene is formed, it undergoes nitration using concentrated $HNO_3$ and $H_2SO_4$.

- **Directing Effect:** The methyl group is an **ortho, para (O, P) directing** group. This means it directs the incoming nitronium ion ($\overset{\oplus}{N}O_2$) specifically to the ortho or para positions.
    
- **Final Product:** Because the goal is **p-nitrotoluene**, the para position is favored due to less steric hindrance compared to the ortho position.

 **Why This Specific Order?**

The order cannot be reversed (Nitration then Alkylation) for two main reasons:

1. **Directing Influence:** If you perform nitration first, you create **nitrobenzene**. The nitro group ($-NO_2$) is a **meta-directing** group. Any subsequent alkylation would place the methyl group in the meta position, resulting in _m-nitrotoluene_ instead of the desired _p-nitrotoluene_.
    
2. **Ring Deactivation:** The nitro group is a strongly **electron-withdrawing group** that deactivates the benzene ring. Friedel-Crafts reactions (like alkylation) generally fail on highly deactivated rings like nitrobenzene because the ring is not nucleophilic enough to attack the carbocation.
