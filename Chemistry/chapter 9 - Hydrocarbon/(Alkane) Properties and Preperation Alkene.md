# **2) Isomerization Rxn**

This reaction involves converting straight-chain alkanes into branched-chain isomers using a catalyst.

**Reaction:**

n-Butane is converted into isobutane in the presence of anhydrous aluminium chloride and hydrogen chloride.

$$\text{CH}_3\text{—CH}_2\text{—CH}_2\text{—CH}_3 \xrightarrow[\text{HCl}]{\text{Anhy. AlCl}_3} \text{CH}_3\text{—CH(CH}_3\text{)—CH}_3$$

### Key Points 

The primary purpose of isomerisation in industry (especially petroleum refining) is to improve fuel quality. As branching increases, several properties change:

- **Branching $\uparrow$**: The process increases the number of branches in the carbon chain.
    
- **Octane Number $\uparrow$**: Branched alkanes have a higher octane rating compared to straight-chain alkanes.
    
- **Fuel Efficiency $\uparrow$**: A higher octane number leads to better combustion efficiency in engines.
    
- **Knocking $\downarrow$**: Increased branching significantly reduces engine "knocking" (premature combustion).

##  Nitration (V.P.N.)

**$\text{Conc}^n \text{ HNO}_3$ vapour phase nitration**

### Left Column: Nitration of Methane

In the case of methane ($\text{CH}_4$), the reaction involves the replacement of a hydrogen atom with a nitro group ($-\text{NO}_2$).

$$\text{CH}_4 \xrightarrow{\text{conc. } \text{HNO}_3} \text{CH}_3\text{-NO}_2$$

**Mechanism Note:**

The image illustrates the interaction between the alkane and nitric acid (represented as $\text{HO-NO}_2$):

$$\text{CH}_3\text{-} \boxed{\text{H \quad HO}} \text{-NO}_2$$

This process involves **C-H Bond Cleavage**.

---

### Right Column: Nitration of Ethane

When higher alkanes like ethane ($\text{CH}_3\text{-}\text{CH}_3$) are used, two types of cleavage can occur, leading to a mixture of products.

$$\text{CH}_3\text{-}\text{CH}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{conc. } \text{HNO}_3} \text{CH}_3\text{-}\text{CH}_2\text{-}\text{NO}_2$$

**1) C-H bond cleavage**

This results in the formation of nitroethane:

$$\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{NO}_2$$

**2) C-C bond cleavage**

At high temperatures in the vapour phase, the carbon-carbon bond can break. This allows the nitro group to attach to the resulting fragments:

$$\text{CH}_3 \: \vdots \: \text{CH}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{NO}_2} \text{CH}_3\text{NO}_2 + \text{CH}_3\text{NO}_2$$

_(Resulting in the formation of nitromethane)_


Vapour phase nitration of alkanes (typically performed at **$400^\circ\text{C}$ to $500^\circ\text{C}$**) is non-selective. It produces a mixture of all possible nitroalkanes obtainable by breaking any $\text{C-H}$ or $\text{C-C}$ bonds in the parent molecule.



$$\text{CH}_3\text{—CH}_2\text{—CH}_3 \xrightarrow{\text{V.P.N.}} \text{Types of products (4)}$$

The image highlights that a total of **4 products** are formed through two different mechanisms:

### a) By C-H bond breaking

The nitro group ($-\text{NO}_2$) replaces a hydrogen atom on either the terminal or central carbon.

- **1-Nitropropane:** $\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{CH}_2\text{NO}_2$
    
- **2-Nitropropane:** $(\text{CH}_3)_2\text{CHNO}_2$
    

### b) By C-C bond breaking

In vapour phase nitration, the carbon-carbon bonds can also cleave, leading to lower-chain nitroalkanes.

- **Nitroethane:** $\text{CH}_3\text{CH}_2\text{NO}_2$ (formed from the ethyl fragment)
    
- **Nitromethane:** $\text{CH}_3\text{NO}_2$ (formed from the methyl fragment)

## Sulphonation

**Reagents:** $\text{Conc. H}_2\text{SO}_4$, fuming $\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4$, or Oleum ($\text{H}_2\text{S}_2\text{O}_7$).

**General Reaction:**

The alkane ($R\text{-H}$) reacts to form an alkane sulphonic acid ($R\text{SO}_3\text{H}$):

$$R\text{—H} \xrightarrow{\text{H}_2\text{SO}_4} R\text{SO}_3\text{H}$$

The reaction mechanism involves the removal of water:

$$R\text{—} \boxed{\text{H} + \text{HO}} \text{—SO}_3\text{H}$$

### Key Points 

1. **Rate of Reaction ($R.O.R$):** Directly proportional ($\alpha$) to the stability of the **Free Radical ($F.R$)**.
    
2. **Feasibility:** The reaction is generally feasible when the alkane has **6 or more than 6 carbons**.
    
3. **Lower Alkanes:** In the case of lower alkanes, it is only feasible when a **$3^\circ$ H (tertiary hydrogen)** is present.
    

**Example:**
Isobutane reacting with oleum and heat ($\Delta$) to form 2-methylpropane-2-sulphonic acid:

$$(\text{CH}_3)_3\text{C—H} \xrightarrow[\Delta]{\text{oleum}} (\text{CH}_3)_3\text{C—SO}_3\text{H}$$
### diff bw them

|**Feature**|**Nitration (V.P.N.)**|**Sulphonation**|
|---|---|---|
|**Reagents**|$\text{Conc. HNO}_3$|$\text{Conc. H}_2\text{SO}_4$ or Oleum|
|**Bond Cleavage**|Both $\text{C-H}$ and $\text{C-C}$|Primarily $\text{C-H}$|
|**Selectivity**|Very low (mixture of products)|Higher (prefers $3^\circ$ H)|
|**Lower Alkanes**|Works for $\text{CH}_4, \text{C}_2\text{H}_6$, etc.|Only works if $3^\circ$ H is present|
