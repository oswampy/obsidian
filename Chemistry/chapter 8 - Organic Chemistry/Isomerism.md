## ** R-S configuration**

- **Rectus:** $C \cdot W$ (Clockwise)
    
- **Sinister:** $A \cdot C \cdot W$ (Anti-Clockwise)
### **F.P.F (Fischer projection formula)**

The notes indicate the orientation of bonds in a Fischer projection:

- **V:** Vertical bonds (point away from the viewer).
    
- **H:** Horizontal bonds (point toward the viewer).

### **Fundamentals of R-S Configuration**

The configuration is determined by the direction of priority (from highest priority 1 to 3):

- **Rectus (R):** Clockwise (C.W) movement.
    
- **Sinister (S):** Anti-Clockwise (A.C.W) movement.

 **1.Fischer Projection Orientation**

In a Fischer projection:

- **V (Vertical):** Bonds point **away** from the viewer.
    
- **H (Horizontal):** Bonds point **toward** the viewer.

**2. Priority Assignment (Cahn-Ingold-Prelog Rules)**

Before determining the configuration, groups are numbered 1 to 4 based on atomic number:
- **1** = Highest atomic number
- **4** = Lowest atomic number (usually Hydrogen)

 **3. Rules for Determining Configuration**

The images outline two specific cases based on the position of the **lowest priority group (4)**.

### **Case 1: If group (4) is on a Vertical position**

- If the sequence **1 → 2 → 3** is **Anti-Clockwise**, the configuration is **S**.
    
- _Example from image:_ $Br$ (1), $OH$ (2), $CH_3$ (3) are arranged such that the path is anti-clockwise, and $H$ (4) is vertical. This results in **S**.
    

### **Case 2: If group (4) is on a Horizontal position**

- When the lowest priority group is horizontal, you determine the apparent direction and then **switch the answer** (palat do).
    
- _Example from image:_
    
    1. Assign priorities: $OH$ (1), $NH_2$ (2), $CH_3$ (3), $H$ (4).
        
    2. The path **1 → 2 → 3** appears **Anti-Clockwise (S)**.
        
    3. Because $H$ (4) is on the horizontal line, "S" is crossed out and changed to **R**.

## Polarimetry

### **Step-by-Step Process**

1. **Light Source:** The process begins with **ordinary light**, which vibrates in all possible planes.
    
2. **Polarization:** This light passes through a **Nicol prism**, which acts as a filter to produce **Plane Polarized Light (P.P.L)**—light that vibrates in only one single plane.
    
3. **Sample Interaction:** The P.P.L is then directed through a sample of the **Optical Substance (O.S)**.

### **Interpreting the Results**

The substance is classified based on whether and how it rotates the plane of the light:

- **Optically Active:** If the P.P.L rotates, the compound is optically active.
    
    - **Dextrorotatory:** The light rotates **Clockwise (C.W)**; denoted as $\theta = +ve$.
        
    - **Levorotatory:** The light rotates **Anti-Clockwise (A.C.W)**; denoted as $\theta = -ve$.
        
- **Optically Inactive:** If the P.P.L **does not rotate**, the compound is considered optically inactive.


## Conditions for Optical Isomerism
1) The ability to rotate light is a condition for **optical isomerism**. A key factor in determining if a molecule will be optically active is whether it lacks a **Plane of Symmetry (P.O.S)**.
- **P.O.S**: A plane that divides a molecule into two equal halves.
    
- If a molecule has a P.O.S (like the letter "A"), it is generally **optically inactive**.
    
- If it lacks a P.O.S (like the letter "F"), it has the potential to be **optically active**.

2) **Center of Symmetry (C.O.S.)**: A central point from which diagonally opposite lines meet the same group at equal distances.(only occurs in **rings** with even no of carbons)

##  Single Chiral Carbon

When a molecule contains **only one chiral carbon**, it is always **optically active**. In this scenario, the relationship between two such molecules can only be one of two things:

1. **Enantiomers**

2. **Identical**
    S->S
    R->R

Enantiomers are defined as **non-superimposable mirror images**.

# Meso Compounds

Meso compounds are compounds which have **two or more chiral centers** but also have **symmetry** (Plane of Symmetry, **P.O.S.** and Center of Symmetry, **C.O.S.**).

### **Key Characteristics**

- **Optically Inactive:** A meso compound is optically inactive due to **internal compensation** or **intramolecular neutralization**.
    
- **Structural Requirement:** It must contain at least 2 chiral carbons.
    

## **D/L System (Relative Configuration)**

This system is primarily used to describe the configuration of mirror images in specific classes of molecules.

### **Rules for Assignment**

1. **Usage:** Applied to biomolecules like **carbohydrates** and **amino acids**.
    
2. **Fischer Projection Setup:** *Place the maximum carbon chain on the **vertical line**.
    
    - The **highest oxidized group** must be at the **top**.
        
    - **Oxidized Group Priority:** $-COOH > -CHO > -CH_2OH$.
        
3. **Determination:** Look at the **last chiral carbon** (farthest from the top):
    
    - If the $-OH$ or $-NH_2$ group is on the **right**, it is **D**.
        
    - If it is on the **left**, it is **L**.
        
4. **Priority Tie-break:** If both $-OH$ and $-NH_2$ are present, the D/L configuration is decided by the position of **$-NH_2$**.
    
5. **Exclusion:** The D/L system is **not used for symmetrical molecules** (meso compounds).
    

* __Important Note__
There is **no relation** between the uppercase **D/L** (configuration) and lowercase **d/l** (optical rotation/dextrorotatory and levorotatory).

## **Racemic Mixture (R.M.)**

**Definition:** An **equimolar mixture** (50% : 50%) of two enantiomers.

- It consists of $50\% \text{ } (+)\text{ or } d$ and $50\% \text{ } (-)\text{ or } l$ isomers.
    
- Unlike meso compounds, racemic mixtures are optically inactive due to **external compensation**.
- Seperation of RM mixture into d and l is called **resolution**

- **Definition:** Molecules with  (continuous) double bonds.
    
- **Optical Activity:** An **even number** of cumulative double bonds can exhibit optical isomerism.

# Calculation of no of Optical Isomers

## i) **For Unsymmetrical Molecules**

For an unsymmetrical molecule with $n$ number of chiral centers:

- **Total Optical Isomers (O.I.)**: $2^{n}$
    
- **Optically Active Isomers**: $2^{n}$
    
- **Meso Isomers**: $0$ (Because symmetry is required for meso)
    
- **Racemic Mixtures (R.M.)**: $\frac{2^{n}}{2} = 2^{n-1}$


## ii)For Symmetrical Molecules
#### **Case 1: $n$ is Even**

Let $p = \frac{n}{2}$.

- **Optically Active Isomers**: $2^{n-1}$
    
- **Meso Isomers**: $2^{p-1}$
    
- **Total Optical Isomers (O.I.)**: $2^{n-1} + 2^{p-1}$
    
- **Racemic Mixtures (R.M.)**: $\frac{\text{Active}}{2}$

#### **Case 2: $n$ is Odd**

Let $p = \frac{n-1}{2}$.

- **Optically Active Isomers**: $2^{n-1} - 2^{p}$
    
- **Meso Isomers**: $2^{p}$
    
- **Total Optical Isomers (O.I.)**: $2^{n-1}$
    
- **Racemic Mixtures (R.M.)**: $\frac{\text{Active}}{2}$
    

 **Quick Reference Table**

|**Chiral Centers (n)**|**Helper Variable (p)**|**Active Isomers**|**Meso Isomers**|**Total Isomers**|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|**Even**|$\frac{n}{2}$|$2^{n-1}$|$2^{p-1}$|$2^{n-1} + 2^{p-1}$|
|**Odd**|$\frac{n-1}{2}$|$2^{n-1} - 2^{p}$|$2^{p}$|$2^{n-1}$|

## **Pseudo Chiral Carbon**

A pseudo chiral carbon is defined by the following characteristics:

1. **Group Differentiation**: It is a carbon atom that has two different groups attached to it.
    
2. **Stereoisomeric Substituents**: The other two groups attached to it are chemically the same but differ in their stereochemical configuration. Specifically:
    
    - They may exhibit **Geometrical Isomerism (G.I.)**.
        
    - Or, they may contain **Chiral Carbons** themselves.

# **OPTICAL ROTATION ($\theta$)**

The optical rotation of a substance depends on the following factors:

- **a) Concentration of solution**: Measured in grams per milliliter ($g/mL$).
    
- **b) Length of the tube**: Measured in decimeters ($dm$).

## **SPECIFIC ROTATION ($\alpha$)**

**Definition**: The rotation observed when the concentration ($C$) of the solution is $1\text{ } g/mL$ and the length of the tube ($L$) is $1\text{ } dm$.

 **Mathematical Formula**

The relationship between specific rotation, observed rotation, concentration, and path length is given by:

$$\alpha = \frac{\theta}{C \cdot L}$$

Where:
- **$\alpha$**: Specific rotation.
- **$\theta$**: Observed optical rotation.
- **$C$**: Concentration of the solution in $g/mL$.
- **$L$**: Length of the polarimeter tube in $dm$.
