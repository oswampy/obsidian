These notes provide a comprehensive overview of Organic Chemistry **Reaction Mechanisms**, focusing on the nature of reagents (electrophiles and nucleophiles) and the basics of nucleophilic substitution.

---

## 1. Reaction Mechanism Basics

In organic chemistry, a reaction typically follows this general path:

$$\text{Substrate (Reactant)} + \text{Reagent} \longrightarrow \text{Product}$$

---

## 2. Electrophiles ($E^+$)

Species that are "electron lovers." They are electron-deficient and attack nucleophilic (electron-rich) sites.

### Types and Examples:

- **Positively Charged:** $Cl^+$, $Br^+$, $CH_3^+$, $H^+$.
    
    - _Note:_ Not all positive species are electrophiles. $NH_4^+$ and $H_3O^+$ have complete octets and only act as electrophiles in aqueous conditions where they can release a proton ($H^+$).
        
- **Incomplete Octet:** $BF_3$, $BCl_3$, $AlCl_3$, $BeCl_2$.
    
    - **Order of Electrophilicity:** $BF_3 < BCl_3 < BBr_3 < BI_3$.
        
    - _Reason:_ In $BF_3$, there is $2p-2p$ back-bonding. In $BI_3$, the $2p-5p$ overlap is weak, making the Boron center more electron-deficient and thus a stronger electrophile.
        
- **Polar Functional Groups:** Species with a partial positive charge ($\delta^+$) on a carbon atom, such as Carbonyls ($C=O$) or Nitriles ($C \equiv N$).
    

### Lewis Acids:

Electron-deficient species that accept a pair of electrons. **All Lewis acids are electrophiles**, but not all electrophiles are Lewis acids (e.g., $CH_3^+$).

---

## 3. Nucleophiles ($Nu^-$)

Species that are "nucleus lovers." They are electron-rich and attack electrophilic centers.

### Types and Examples:

- **Negatively Charged:** $Cl^-$, $Br^-$, $NH_2^-$, $OH^-$, $CH_3^-$, $C_2H_5O^-$.
    
- **Neutral (with lone pairs):** $H_2O$, $NH_3$, $R\text{-}OH$, $R\text{-}NH_2$.
    
- **Organometallic Compounds:** $R\text{-}MgX$ (Grignard Reagent), $R_2Zn$ (Frankland Reagent), $R_2CuLi$ (Gilman's Reagent), $R\text{-}Li$.
    
- **$\pi$-electron rich species:** Alkenes ($C=C$), Alkynes ($C \equiv C$), and Benzene.
    

### Nucleophile vs. Lewis Base:

- **Lewis Base:** Attacks an $H^+$ (proton).
    
- **Nucleophile:** Attacks any electrophilic center other than $H^+$.
    

---

## 4. Periodic Trends

### Group 15 Hydrides ($NH_3$ to $SbH_3$):

- **Basic Character:** $NH_3 > PH_3 > AsH_3 > SbH_3$. (Small nitrogen holds lone pairs more densely; $Sb$ is bulky and donation is harder).
    
- **Nucleophilic Character:** $NH_3 < PH_3 < AsH_3 < SbH_3$.
    

### Period 2 Species ($CH_3^-$ to $F^-$):

- **Basic & Nucleophilic Character:** $CH_3^- > NH_2^- > OH^- > F^-$.
    
    - _Reason:_ Electronegativity (EN) increases from C to F. Higher EN means the atom holds onto its negative charge more tightly, making it less likely to donate electrons.
        

---

## 5. Nucleophilic Substitution ($S_N$)

General reaction:

$$Nu^- + R\text{-}X \longrightarrow R\text{-}Nu + X^-$$

Here, $X^-$ is the **Leaving Group (L.G.)**.

### Leaving Group Ability:

The rate of reaction is proportional to the stability of the leaving group. A weaker base is a better leaving group.

- **Halogens:** $I^- > Br^- > Cl^- > F^-$.
    
    - $HI$ is a very strong acid, meaning $I^-$ is a very weak, stable base and thus the **best** leaving group.
        
- **Stability:** $OH^- > NH_2^-$ because Oxygen is more electronegative than Nitrogen and can stabilize the negative charge better.
