

## ALCOHOLS, PHENOLS AND ETHERS

---

# 🔹 1. Alcohols

## Structure

Alcohols contain a hydroxyl group (–OH) attached to an sp³ hybridised carbon.

General formula:  
R–OH

---

## Classification

### Based on number of –OH groups

- Monohydric (1 –OH)
    
- Dihydric (2 –OH)
    
- Trihydric (3 –OH)
    

### Based on carbon type

- **Primary (1°)** → R–CH₂–OH
    
- **Secondary (2°)** → R₂CH–OH
    
- **Tertiary (3°)** → R₃C–OH
    

---

## Nomenclature

- Replace “-e” of alkane with “-ol”
    
- Number chain to give –OH lowest number
    

Example:  
CH₃CH₂OH → ethanol

---

## Preparation of Alcohols

### 1. From Alkenes

**Acid-catalysed hydration**  
Follows Markovnikov’s rule

CH₂=CH₂ + H₂O → CH₃CH₂OH

---

### 2. Hydroboration–Oxidation

Anti-Markovnikov addition

Alkene → alcohol (–OH to less substituted carbon)

---

### 3. From Haloalkanes

RX + KOH (aq) → ROH + KX

Mechanism:

- SN1 (tertiary)
    
- SN2 (primary)
    
---

### 4. Reduction of Carbonyl Compounds

- Aldehyde → 1° alcohol
    
- Ketone → 2° alcohol
    

Reducing agents:

- NaBH₄
    
- LiAlH₄
    

---

### 5. Grignard Reagent

RMgX + H₂O → RH + Mg(OH)X

With carbonyl:

- HCHO → 1° alcohol
    
- Aldehyde → 2° alcohol
    
- Ketone → 3° alcohol
    

---

## Physical Properties

### Hydrogen Bonding

- Intermolecular H-bonding → high boiling point
    
- Stronger than ethers
    

### Solubility

- Lower alcohols → soluble in water
    
- Solubility ↓ with chain length
    

---

## Chemical Reactions

---

### 1. Reaction with Metals

2ROH + 2Na → 2RONa + H₂

---

### 2. Reaction with HX

ROH + HX → RX + H₂O

Order:  
3° > 2° > 1°

---

### 3. Dehydration

Alcohol → Alkene

Conditions:

- Conc. H₂SO₄
    
- 443 K
    

Mechanism:

- 2° & 3° → E1
    
- 1° → E2
    

---

### 4. Oxidation

#### Primary alcohol

RCH₂OH → RCHO → RCOOH

#### Secondary alcohol

R₂CHOH → R₂CO

#### Tertiary alcohol

No oxidation (no α-H)

---

### 5. Esterification

ROH + RCOOH ⇌ RCOOR + H₂O

Catalyst: H₂SO₄

---

### 6. Lucas Test

Reagent: ZnCl₂ + HCl

- 3° → immediate turbidity
    
- 2° → few minutes
    
- 1° → no turbidity
    

---

# 🔹 2. Phenols

## Structure

–OH attached directly to benzene ring

Ar–OH

---

## Preparation

### 1. From Haloarenes (Dow’s Process)

Chlorobenzene + NaOH (623 K, 300 atm) → Sodium phenoxide → Phenol

---

### 2. From Benzene Sulphonic Acid

Fusion with NaOH → phenol

---

### 3. From Diazonium Salt

ArN₂⁺ + H₂O → ArOH + N₂

---

### 4. Cumene Process

Industrial method

Cumene → cumene hydroperoxide → phenol + acetone

---

## Acidic Nature

Phenol is acidic due to **resonance stabilization of phenoxide ion**

Order:  
Phenol > Alcohol

Electron-withdrawing groups ↑ acidity  
Electron-donating groups ↓ acidity

---

## Reactions of Phenol

---

### 1. With Na

ArOH + Na → ArONa + H₂
ArOH+NaOH->ArONa + H2O


### **a. Reaction with $NaNH_2$:**

$$PhOH + Na^+NH_2^- \longrightarrow NH_3 + PhO^-Na^+$$
Phenol ($pKa \approx 10$) is acidic enough to react with **Sodamide** ($NaNH_2$), which is an extremely strong base. The amide ion ($NH_2^-$) abstracts a proton from phenol to form ammonia and sodium phenoxide.
### **b. Reaction with $NaHCO_3$:**

-**No RXN** (No Reaction)
	Phenol is a **weaker acid** than carbonic acid ($H_2CO_3$, $pKa \approx 6.4$). Therefore, sodium bicarbonate ($NaHCO_3$) is not a strong enough base to deprotonate phenol, resulting in no reaction. This is often used as a laboratory test to distinguish phenols from carboxylic acids.

---

### 2. Electrophilic Substitution

–OH activates ring

Positions: ortho & para

---

### 3. Halgonation
-Bromination
Phenol + Br₂/h20 → 2,4,6-tribromophenol (white ppt)
Phenol + Br₂/CS2 → para or ortho bromophenol

---

### 4. Nitration

Dilute HNO₃ → o- and p-nitrophenol

Conc. HNO₃ → picric acid

---

### 5. Kolbe Reaction

Phenoxide + CO₂+NaOH → salicylic acid
salicylic acid+CH3COOCOCH3-> aspirin (MOP)

---

### 6. Reimer–Tiemann Reaction

Phenol + CHCl₃ + NaOH → salicylaldehyde (ortho major because of H-bonding)

---

### 7. Oxidation

Phenol +Na2cr2o7→ quinone (no tautomerism)

---

# 🔹 3. Ethers

## Structure

R–O–R′

---

## Nomenclature

- Alkoxyalkane system
    

Example:  
CH₃–O–CH₃ → methoxymethane

---

## Preparation

### 1. Williamson Ether Synthesis
SN2-
R–O⁻ + R′X → R–O–R′

Best for primary halides

---

### 2. Dehydration of Alcohols

2ROH +conc h2so4 at (413K)→ R–O–R + H₂O

---

## Physical Properties

- No H-bonding between molecules
    
- Lower boiling point than alcohols
    
- Slightly soluble in water
    

---

## Chemical Reactions

---

### 1. Cleavage with HX

R–O–R′ + HX → RX + ROH

Order:  
HI > HBr > HCl

Mechanism:

- SN1 or SN2 depending on structure

#### ** Reaction with excess $HX$**

 **1. Cleavage of Anisole (Methyl Phenyl Ether)**

In the first reaction, an alkyl aryl ether reacts with excess $HX$.

**Equation:**

$$Ph-O-CH_3 \xrightarrow{\text{Excess } HX} PhOH + CH_3X$$

**Explanation:**

Even with **excess** $HX$, the reaction stops at **Phenol** ($PhOH$) and methyl halide ($CH_3X$). This is because the $C_{sp^2}-O$ bond in phenol has partial double-bond character due to resonance, making it extremely difficult to break. Consequently, phenol does not react further to form a halobenzene.

---

#### **2. Cleavage of Aliphatic Ethers**

In the second reaction, a simple aliphatic ether (Ethyl Methyl Ether) is treated with excess $HX$.

**Equation:**

$$CH_3-O-CH_2CH_3 \xrightarrow{\text{Excess } HX} CH_3X + CH_3CH_2X$$

**Explanation:**

Unlike the aryl example, both carbon-oxygen bonds in an aliphatic ether are single bonds ($C_{sp^3}-O$). When $HX$ is in **excess**:

1. The ether is first cleaved into an alcohol and an alkyl halide.
    
2. The resulting alcohol then reacts with the remaining $HX$ to form a **second alkyl halide**.
    
3. The final products are two alkyl halides and water ($H_2O$).
    
 **Key Rule for Ether Cleavage** ----

- **With 1 equivalent of $HX$:** You get one alcohol and one alkyl halide. The halide usually attacks the smaller/less hindered alkyl group (via $S_N2$).
    
- **With excess $HX$:**
    
    - **Aliphatic ethers:** Yield **two alkyl halides**.
        
    - **Aryl ethers:** Yield **one phenol and one alkyl halide** (phenol remains unreacted).

---

### 2. Auto oxidation

Ethers form peroxides on storage

(dangerous)
