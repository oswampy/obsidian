## Types of Redox Rxns

### 1) **Disproportionation Rxn:**

One element in a single oxidation state gets oxidized and Reduced in same rxn. → +1 O.D

**ex:** $$2\text{H}_2\text{O}_2 \rightarrow 2\text{H}_2\text{O} + \text{O}_2$$

(with -1 R.D shown above)

Atom present at highest or lowest O.N cannot show Disp. Rxn

### 2) **Comproportionation / synproportionation**

Two species of same element but different O.N react to  form a product with intermediate ion

$$\text{Cu}^{0} + \text{Cu}^{+2} \rightarrow 2\text{Cu}^{+}$$

## Electrode Potential (E)

When a metal rod is dipped into a $\text{sol}^n$ of its own ions, a tendency develops for the metals to: lose $e^-$ or gain $e^-$. This potential tendency creates potential difference b/w metal and $\text{sol}^n$. This is electrode potential.

## Oxidation Potential

Tendency of electrode to lose $e^-$

$$\text{Zn} \rightarrow 2e^- + \text{Zn}^{2+}$$

## Reduction Potential

Tendency of electrode to gain $e^-$

$$\text{Ca}^{2+} + 2e^- \rightarrow \text{Cu}$$

## • Standard electrode potential $(E°)$ is measured under SATP condition (1 atm, 25°C, 1M)

→ $E°$ is measured by SHE/NHE (Standard Hydrogen Electrode). SHE has $E^o$ potential of 0 so it can be +ve or -ve

→ SHE is a reference electrode. Measurement of absolute electrode potential is not possible.
