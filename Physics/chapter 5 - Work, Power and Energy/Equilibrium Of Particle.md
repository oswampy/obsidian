# **Equilibrium of particles** 
If $F_{\text{net}} = 0$, a particle is said to be in equilibrium.
$W_c = -\Delta U$

$F_c \cdot \Delta r = -\Delta U$

$F_c = -\dfrac{\Delta U}{\Delta r}$
For a small displacement:  
$$F_c = -\dfrac{dU}{dr}$$

-Negative of derivative of P.E w.r.t position $(r)$ gives the conservative force acting on the particle at that point.
## **Types of Equilibrium**

1. Stable Equilibrium: When P.E of the particle is minimum and force acting on it is $0$, the particle is said to be in stable equilibrium. If this following equation satisfies, then particle is in equilibrium,so we check the type:  (if not, particle is not in equilibrium) 
    $$F = -\dfrac{dU}{dr} = 0$$
	$\dfrac{d^2U}{dr^2} \bigg|_{r_0} > 0$ → energy is minimum → stable equilibrium.
	
2. Unstable Equilibrium:  
    $\dfrac{d^2U}{dr^2} \bigg|_{r_0} < 0$ → energy is maximum → unstable equilibrium.
    
3. Neutral Equilibrium:  
    $\dfrac{d^2U}{dr^2} \bigg|_{r_0} = 0$ → energy is constant → neutral equilibrium.

At point A, if we displace the point by a small distance $\Delta x$: It is already at its highest energy(stable equilibrium), so any displacement will reduce $U$ (i.e. $dU < 0$)
$F = -\dfrac{dU}{dx}$

 If displacement is $+\Delta x$, then $dx > 0$ and $\dfrac{dU}{dx} < 0$, so $F > 0$, meaning force is in direction of motion.
 
 this is opposite at point B where F < 0 which means force is against motion
