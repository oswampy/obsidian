
In our daily life, rigid body is not completely rigid. On application of some force on it, its shape and size will change. To resist the change in its shape or size, it will apply a resistance force opposite to external deformation force.

-**Elasticity**

Definition: The property of material by which it regains its shape and size when deformation force is removed.

Example: Steel is more elastic than rubber.

**-Plasticity**

Definition: The property of material by which it will not regain its original shape and size even if external force is removed.

# Stress

When we apply force on material and it resists with equal and opposite force, that force per unit area is called stress.

    $\text{Stress} = \frac{\text{Force}}{\text{Area}}$

S.I. unit is Pa (N/m²)

Stress will only develop when applied in pairs. Common part of force will provide stress.

Types of Stress

1) Longitudinal Stress

When stress is applied to change length of any object, it is called longitudinal stress. 

If it tries to increase length, it is called tensile strength.

If it tries to decrease length, it is called compressive stress.

2) Shear Stress

When force is applied on any object to bend it, the stress developed is called shear stress.

3) Hydraulic Stress / Volumetric Stress

When we apply stress to change volume of any object, we call it volumetric stress.

    $\text{H. Stress} = \frac{F}{A}$

# Strain

When we apply stress on any object, the size of object changes. The ratio of change in size to original size is called strain.

Types of Strain

1) Longitudinal Strain

Change in length divided by original length:

    $\text{Longitudinal Strain} = \frac{\Delta L}{L}$

2) Shear Strain

    $\frac{\Delta L}{L} = \tan\theta = \text{shear stress}$

When we apply shear stress, the angle by which object bends is shear strain.

3) Volume Strain

When we apply hydraulic stress to change volume of any object:

    $\frac{\Delta V}{V} = \text{volume strain}$

**Hooke's Law**

Within an elastic limit:

    $\text{Stress} \propto \text{Strain}$

Strain is developed by force and stress is developed by strain.

## Behavior Regions:

### 1) Elastic Behaviour (O to A)

- **OA**: The material will show elastic behaviour, it will  follow Hooke's law; the stress-strain curve is no more linear

### 2) Strain Hardening (A to B)

- **AB**: The material will show elastic behaviour, but it will not follow Hooke's law; the stress-strain curve is no more linear

### 3) Plastic Deformation (B to C)

- **BC**: If we increase strain further after point B, the stress will start to decrease. Here (B) plastic deformation starts and at point C, stress reaches its minimum value. Here at B to C, material dimensions will be changed easily

### 4) Strain Hardening/Necking (C to D)

- **CD**: If we increase strain further after point C, the stress will again start to increase due to internal strengthening and at point D, stress will reach its maximum value. In this section, the material will show plastic behaviour, but it will become stronger

### 5) Fracture (D to E)

- **DE**: This is called breaking or fracture point. Here, cross-section of material decreases rapidly and then breaks
