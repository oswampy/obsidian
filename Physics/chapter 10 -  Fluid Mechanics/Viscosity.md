

When a fluid flows, layers of the fluid slide over each other. Just like solid, these layers also experience frictional force opposite to relative motion. This force is called viscous force.

-dia
	![[viscosity_exp.excalidraw]]

The velocity of the layer which is in contact with the bottom surface will be at rest and as we go up velocity will increase.

The viscous force is directly proportional to area of layer and velocity gradient.

$$F_v \propto A \quad \text{—(i)}$$

$$F_v \propto \frac{dv}{dy} \quad \text{—(ii)}$$

$$F_v = -\eta A \frac{dv}{dy}$$

where $\eta$ is coefficient of viscosity and negative sign indicates opposite direction.

---


• Viscosity and friction are similar in the way they both oppose relative motion between two adjacent surfaces in contact. Both due to molecular attraction.

• Viscosity and friction are different in the way viscosity depends on area and velocity, where friction is independent of both. Friction depends on normal between 2 surfaces where viscosity will not.

Unit of $\eta = \text{kg m}^{-1}\text{s}^{-1}$

As temperature increases, viscosity decreases

$$\eta \propto \frac{1}{\sqrt{T}}$$

## **Stokes Law**

The viscous force on a spherical body of radius $r$ in a fluid of coefficient of viscosity $\eta$ is:

$$F = 6\pi \eta r v$$

**Terminal Velocity**

When a body is dropped in a fluid, it accelerates first then its acceleration becomes 0 then attains a constant velocity called terminal velocity.
